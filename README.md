# Cold DM Skill

A reusable skill for writing cold and warm outreach DMs that actually get replies, plus the thank-you notes, follow-ups, and post-comments around them. The full playbook lives in `SKILL.md`. This README covers what's here, how to use it, and the part that makes or breaks every message: **how to research a person and read their work before you write a word.**

## What's in here

- `SKILL.md` — the playbook: the three-sentence formula, what works, what to cut, accuracy rules, the editing loop, recipient calibration, and sequencing.
- `README.md` — this file.

## How to use it

**As a Claude skill:** keep the folder structure (`cold-dm/SKILL.md`) and install or load it. The description in the frontmatter triggers it whenever you ask for outreach help or paste a profile.

**As a plain reference:** open `SKILL.md` and follow it by hand. Paste a target's profile and your own background into any chat and ask for a draft against the formula.

**Either way, the workflow is:**
1. Research the person (see below).
2. Draft against the three-sentence formula in `SKILL.md`.
3. Run the cutting passes until every sentence earns its place.
4. Send the small ask (a 15-minute chat). Escalate later.

## How to research a person and read their work

A cold DM only works if the hook is **specific, real, and recent**. That comes from research, not guessing. Generic praise ("I admire your work") fails because anyone could write it. A line that quotes their actual finding from a paper they wrote years ago can't be faked, and that's exactly why it lands.

Budget more time on research than on writing. The message is short; the homework is the job.

### Step 1: Read the whole profile, not just the headline

Go past the current title. Pull from:

- **Their "about" / bio** — the throughline they chose to tell about themselves.
- **Full work history** — the *order* of moves matters. A pivot, a return, a jump between fields, time spent founding something. The shape of a career is often the best hook.
- **Education and side details** — a thesis topic, a fellowship, a patent, a sport, a second language. Specific and rarely mentioned by others.
- **Volunteering / causes** — often where the genuine mission lives.

Write down the 2-3 most specific, distinctive, real details. These are your hook candidates.

### Step 2: Read what they've actually published or posted

This is the highest-value research and the part most people skip.

- **Recent posts** — what are they talking about *right now*? React to a current idea, not a stale one.
- **Articles, papers, reports, whitepapers** — find the actual document and read it. Pull a specific finding, statistic, or argument. Quoting a real result ("X% prefer A over B") is far stronger than summarizing vibes.
- **Older work** — dig back years. Referencing something from early in their career signals effort almost nobody makes, and flatters without grovelling.
- **Talks, podcasts, panels** — sometimes the most candid, quotable material.

When you read their work, look for one of two things:
1. A finding striking enough to react to honestly.
2. A problem or gap in their work that overlaps with a gap *you've* hit in yours. That intellectual overlap is the strongest hook of all: same problem, two vantage points.

#### Where to actually find their work (search tactics)

Finding the material is its own skill. What works, roughly in order:

- **Comb their LinkedIn posts AND reposts.** The Activity tab (Posts, Comments, Documents) shows what they care about right now. Reposts are a tell too: what they amplify says as much as what they write. Don't forget the "Featured" section, where people pin their most important work.
- **Search their name + their firm or school.** A plain web search like `[Full Name] [Firm or School]` surfaces bios, articles, interviews, and announcements that never appear on their profile.
- **Search for published work and talks specifically.** Add a format or content word: `[Full Name] [Firm] PDF`, or swap in `talk`, `podcast`, `interview`, `panel`, `webinar`, `keynote`. PDFs are often papers, whitepapers, or slide decks with quotable specifics.
- **Use search operators to narrow.** `"Full Name"` in quotes forces an exact match. `[Full Name] filetype:pdf` finds documents. `site:firm.com [Full Name]` finds their employer's own bio/author page (consulting firms, universities, and labs all maintain these, often with a fuller history than LinkedIn).
- **Go to the source databases for researchers.** Google Scholar, ResearchGate, ORCID, and PubMed surface papers, co-authors, and citation trails. A faculty or staff profile page is often the richest single source.
- **Find spoken material.** Search YouTube and podcast platforms by name; conference agendas and speaker pages list talks (and affiliations). Spoken content is often the most candid and quotable.
- **Find where they're quoted.** News articles, press releases, and company blog posts that quote them give you current, attributable lines to react to.
- **Check personal channels.** A Substack, Medium, personal site, or X/Twitter account is sometimes where the realest thinking lives.

#### How to find their email

- **The cleanest source: their own published work.** Papers and reports almost always list a **corresponding author** or a point-of-contact email. Whitepapers and articles frequently print the author's work email at the end.
- **Deduce the firm's email pattern.** Once you have one known address from a paper, you know the firm's format (e.g., `first_last@`, `first.last@`, `flast@`). Apply that pattern to anyone else at the same firm.
- **Faculty/staff pages and conference listings** often print emails directly.
- **A warm intro still beats a cold email.** If a mutual contact can introduce you, take that over any address you dig up, especially for recruiters.

### Step 3: Find the genuine connection

The hook has to tie to the sender, not just flatter the recipient. Look for the real overlap:

- Same field, problem, or mission.
- A path that rhymes with the sender's (a similar pivot, a shared background, a parallel move).
- A finding in their work that maps onto the sender's own experience.

If there's no real connection, this may be the wrong person to message. A great message to the right person beats ten generic ones.

### Step 4: Verify everything before it goes in

Research is worthless if the detail is wrong, and a single error undercuts the whole message. Before writing:

- **Check the verb.** Did they *found*, *lead*, or *join* the thing? They're different claims.
- **Check the story.** Does the timeline actually support the arc you're about to describe (a return, a pivot, a sequence)?
- **Check identity.** If you're referencing a co-author or a name, confirm it's the same person and not someone with a matching first name.
- **Check the specifics.** Country, dataset, dates, statistics. If you cite a published finding, cite it exactly.

If a detail can't be verified, leave it out. A vaguer true message beats a specific false one.

### Step 5: Match the research to the recipient

How deep to go, and what to surface, depends on who they are:

- **Senior leaders / partners** — find their published work or a signature idea; build one sharp question around it.
- **Recruiters** — research the *hiring* context (office, role, timing) more than personal detail; ask a tactical question.
- **Peers / mid-level** — the career-path or shared-background hook usually works; recent posts and the shape of their moves are enough.

## The core idea

The research is what separates a message that gets ignored from one that gets answered. The formula in `SKILL.md` is simple on purpose. The work is reading carefully enough to say something only this person could receive, and only this sender could send.
