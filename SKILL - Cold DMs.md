# Cold DMs That Get Responses

A skill for turning a stranger's public profile into a short message they actually answer. It captures a tested process: draft against a formula, then run cutting passes until every sentence earns its place.

The goal of a cold DM is never the big thing (a job, a referral). It's one small, easy "yes": a 15-minute chat. Everything else comes later.

---

## The three-sentence formula

Every message has three moves, in order:

1. **Hook** — one or more specific, real details from their profile/posts/published work, plus a genuine human reaction. Proves you looked. Earns the read.
2. **Who you are + what drives you** — one tight line connecting you to *them*.
3. **One small ask** — a single, specific, answerable question personal to them, then "Would you be open to a 15 min chat?"

Warm one-word sign-off. That's the whole message.

Structure, not script:

> Hi [Name],
> [One or two specific real details from their work] + [a short human reaction].
> [One sentence: who the sender is + the thread that ties them to this person].
> [One specific, answerable question aimed at them]. Open to a 15 min chat?
> [First name]

---

## What works (do these)

**Stack 2-3 real, specific details, then react like a human.** The specificity is the credibility; the reaction is the warmth. Generic praise ("your impressive work") does neither. Pull concrete nouns from their actual profile, not adjectives about them.

**Quote an actual finding from their own work and connect it to the sender.** Naming a specific statistic or result from something they wrote, then linking it to the sender's own experience, was the highest-performing opening style. It proves real engagement and makes the overlap intellectual rather than flattering.

**Dig up their older or less-obvious work.** Referencing something from years back signals effort almost nobody else makes. It flatters without being obsequious.

**Find the genuine intellectual overlap.** The strongest hook connects a problem or finding in *their* work to a real gap the sender hit in *their own* work. Same problem, two vantage points.

**Right-size the question so it's answerable fast.** A question they can answer in two sentences gets a reply. A grand, essay-sized question gets skipped because it needs a prepared answer.

**Calibrate the ask to seniority** (see below).

---

## What to cut (anti-patterns)

When a draft drifts into one of these, rewrite it.

**Cleverness for its own sake.** Cute metaphors, wordplay, and self-satisfied turns of phrase read as trying too hard (or as AI). Replace a clever line with a plain, honest reaction. Plain and human wins every time.

**Personality that overwhelms a hook meant to be low-key.** A vivid, intense observation can be too much for a first message. Soften it or drop it.

**Roundabout or grandiose questions.** Abstract "big think" questions feel like assigned homework. Swap for something concrete and immediate about their current work or team.

**About-the-sender instead of about-the-recipient.** The hook is *their* work, *their* path, *their* idea. The sender's own story is one line of connective tissue, never the centerpiece. If a draft drifts into the sender's biography, swing it back.

**Long sentences.** Break them into fragments. Punch beats flow.

**Length overall.** Length is not depth. Cut back to the three-move formula.

---

## Accuracy (one wrong detail kills the message)

Senior people notice sloppiness, and a single wrong detail undercuts everything.

- **Use the true verb.** Founding, leading, and joining are different claims. Don't write "built" when the truth is "led" or "joined."
- **Verify the story.** Don't assert a career arc (a return, a pivot, a sequence) the timeline doesn't actually support.
- **Don't conflate similar names.** If a co-author or contact shares a first name with someone else, confirm they're the same person before referencing them.
- **Get specifics exactly right.** Country, dataset, role, dates, statistics. If you cite a published finding, cite it precisely.

If a detail can't be verified, leave it out. A vaguer true message beats a specific false one.

## The editing loop (how the good version gets made)

Cold DMs are rarely one-shot. The reliable process:

1. Read the profile. Pull the 2-3 most specific, distinctive, real details.
2. Pick the single strongest hook, ideally one tied to the sender's own background.
3. Draft against the three-sentence formula.
4. Run cutting passes, each answering one question:
   - Anything clever for its own sake? → cut it.
   - Any long sentence? → fragment it.
   - About them or about the sender? → swing it back to them.
   - Question answerable in two sentences? → shrink it if not.
   - Any em dashes? → replace with commas, periods, or parentheses.
   - Every factual claim verified? → fix verbs, names, numbers.
5. Match tone and ask to the recipient's seniority.
6. Deliver, then offer to tighten or swap the question.

Expect several rounds: shorten, make it warmer, smooth the flow, verify a claim. That iteration is the method, not a failure.

## Hard rules

- **No em dashes.** Commas, periods, parentheses instead.
- **Short sentences. Fragments welcome.**
- **About them, not the sender.**
- **Only hook off things genuinely in their profile.** Never reference an interest, paper, or overlap that can't be backed up live.
- **The ask stays small.** A 15-minute chat. Never ask for a job or referral in a cold message.
- **Warm, chill, professional.** No filler openers, no groveling.

---

## Calibrate to the recipient

- **Senior leaders / partners.** One sharp, slightly bold question tied to their life's work beats a safe generic one. They get flooded, so the specific hook and small ask matter most. Lower reply odds; that's expected. Never ask for a referral cold.
- **Recruiters.** Not a coffee-chat contact. Keep it professional and concise, and ask a *tactical, specific* question they can answer fast (e.g., whether a given office or role hires on a rolling basis or in windows). An intro from a mutual contact beats cold-messaging a recruiter.
- **Peers / mid-level.** Often the *highest* reply rate. The pivot-story or shared-background hook lands well; tone can be slightly more casual.

---

## Sequencing: the ask escalates over time

- **Cold DM:** ask only for a 15-minute chat.
- **Warm-up move:** a short, thoughtful comment on a recent post puts the sender's name on the recipient's radar so the DM lands warmer.
- **After the reply / after the call:** *that's* when the bigger ask comes (resume review, referral, intro to someone else). It lands because they now know the sender.
- **Name-dropping:** only invoke a mutual contact if they explicitly authorized it. A referral name to a recruiter is powerful but only once the referral is actually *submitted*, not merely promised. Momentum can be signaled without names ("I've been speaking with people across the [X] team").

---

## Thank-you notes and follow-ups

- Send within 24 hours. Thank them, name **one specific takeaway** (proves you listened), keep a door open.
- A soft line like "if there's anyone else you think I should connect with" is how intros happen organically.
- This is the moment for the bigger ask if there is one.
- Don't over-follow-up. Reaching out with no real update reads as desperate. Make follow-ups organic: a genuine progress update, or a relevant article tied to something they care about.

---

## Quick checklist before sending

- [ ] Opens with a specific, real detail about them (not generic praise)
- [ ] Human reaction, not a clever line
- [ ] One tight sentence about the sender
- [ ] One answerable, specific question
- [ ] Ends on "15 min chat?"
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] Short sentences
- [ ] Every fact verified (verbs, names, numbers)
- [ ] Reads like a person talking, not a cover letter
